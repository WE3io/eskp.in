## Preamble: Research Scope and Confidence Ratings

This document produces findings across seven domains: regulated advice exclusions, high-risk adjacent domains, sensitive handling protocols, directory concept specification, directory revenue models, failure mode analysis, and academic/ethical framing. Every section terminates in either a testable feature prototype specification or a failure mode entry.

Confidence ratings are explicit throughout. Where UK law is contested or unclear, this is flagged rather than resolved artificially. Web research encountered significant access restrictions across regulatory body websites; findings draw on primary legislation (accessed via legislation.gov.uk), statutory instruments, and secondary sources where primary sources were unavailable. All legislative citations are to enacted versions current as of early 2026.

**A note on the platform's OSA exposure:** The Online Safety Act 2023 defines a "user-to-user service" as any internet service by which content generated by one user may be encountered by another user. The eskp.in dyadic model — where Panel Member A sees User messages and User sees Panel Member A messages, but Panel Member A cannot see Panel Member B's thread — likely constitutes a regulated user-to-user service. The internal business service exemption (Schedule 1) requires the service to be available only to a closed group of employees, officers, or authorised contractors of a business; this exemption does not apply to a general-purpose advisory platform. The platform should assume OSA applies and plan accordingly. Ofcom enforcement is proportionate to scale, but the legal duties exist regardless of platform size.

## Section 1: Regulated Domain Exclusion Register

### Legal Framework Overview

The UK operates a patchwork of profession-specific regulatory regimes. There is no single "advice regulation" statute. Liability arises from multiple sources:

1. **Statutory prohibition** — carrying on a specified activity without authorisation is a criminal offence
2. **Reserved activities** — specific acts (e.g., litigation, conveyancing) may only be performed by authorised persons
3. **Protected titles** — using a protected professional title without registration is an offence
4. **Common law negligence** — a duty of care may arise even for informal advice if a reasonable person would rely on it (see *Hedley Byrne v Heller* [1964] AC 465)
5. **Consumer protection** — misleading advice may give rise to liability under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008

**Platform liability vs individual liability:** Under OSA 2023 s.184, platforms that transmit communications are explicitly exempted from the offence of encouraging serious self-harm: "A provider of an internet service by means of which a communication is sent, transmitted or published is not to be regarded as a person who sends, transmits or publishes it." This provides partial protection. However, it does not protect a platform that actively curates, promotes, or facilitates advice exchanges in regulated domains. The safer position is that platform liability risk is real where the platform: (a) knows advice in a regulated domain is being given; (b) takes no steps to prevent or caveat it; and (c) profits from the exchange. This is an unsettled area and the platform should treat it as live risk.

## Section 2: High-Risk Adjacency Register

These domains are not (or not entirely) legally regulated in the same way as Section 1, but carry meaningful risk of harm from poor informal advice. The "hard exclusion vs sensitive handling" distinction is applied explicitly.

### Framework: Active Advice vs Witnessed Reflection

Before the register, a critical distinction needs to be established. Several therapeutic and coaching frameworks have operationalised the difference between:

**Active advice:** "You should do X. Based on what you've told me, X is the right choice for you." — The panel member takes a directive position, assumes responsibility for the outcome, and shapes the user's decision.

**Witnessed reflection:** "I hear that you're going through Y. What feels right to you? What does part of you want to do?" — The panel member holds space, reflects back, asks questions, and helps the user access their own judgment.

This distinction maps onto:
- **Coaching** (goal-directed, future-focused, assumes the client is resourceful and capable) — closer to witnessed reflection
- **Mentoring** (experience-sharing, guidance from one who has walked a similar path) — can be either
- **Counselling/therapy** (processing past experience, clinical formulation, symptom management) — professional domain

The platform should, through panel member onboarding and in-platform prompts, actively cultivate the witnessed reflection mode. This is not just ethical risk management — it is better for users. A panel member who witnesses and reflects is helping the user build their own judgment. A panel member who advises directionally is creating dependency.

**Testable prototype:** Panel member onboarding module with scenario-based training on the active advice/witnessed reflection distinction. Pilot with five panel members; measure whether their message content shifts toward reflective prompting after training. Success criterion: >60% reduction in directive phrasing ("you should," "I recommend," "the right thing is") in messages after training.

## Section 3: Sensitive Handling Protocol

### 3.1 Decision Framework for Surfacing Professional Signposting

The core principle is: **the platform must not abandon the user at the moment of disclosure**. Cold signposting — an automated message listing helpline numbers immediately after a concerning disclosure — has documented poor uptake and can feel dismissive or alienating (Patel et al., 2018, *Crisis* journal; several systematic reviews on mental health help-seeking indicate that abrupt redirection to professionals reduces rather than increases help-seeking behaviour).

**Warm referral vs cold signposting:**
- **Cold signposting:** "We notice your message may relate to [topic]. Please contact [service] on [number]." Delivered impersonally. Evidence suggests this is often ignored.
- **Warm referral:** The person making the referral maintains the relationship while introducing the specialist. "I'm here with you in this. And I also want to make sure you have access to the specific support this deserves — have you spoken to [service]?" Evidence from primary care and peer support literature indicates warm referral significantly increases the probability of the referred person accessing help (Katon & Unützer, 2013; collaborative care literature generally).

The platform's dyadic architecture creates a specific constraint: the platform operator cannot monitor the content of bilateral threads (privacy-by-design). This means the **platform cannot intercept a crisis disclosure and add signposting without disrupting the dyadic trust**. The signposting mechanisms must therefore be:

1. **Built into panel member onboarding** — panel members are trained to provide warm referral themselves
2. **Available as a platform-surfaced resource panel members can invoke** — a "send a resource" function that allows a panel member to share a signposting card within the thread, in their own voice
3. **Available as a user-initiated resource** — a persistent "get more help" panel visible to users at all times
4. **Triggered by in-platform keyword detection only with user consent** — if the platform does scan for crisis keywords, this should be disclosed in the privacy policy and the response should be a gentle, non-alarming prompt, not an automated intervention that interrupts the conversation

**Dyadic-specific crisis constraint:** If a panel member discloses something serious about *themselves* (not the user) in a bilateral thread — for example, a panel member discloses suicidal ideation — the platform has a harder problem. The user is not trained to respond to this. The platform should:
- Include guidance in panel member onboarding that they should seek their own support outside the platform if they are in distress
- Include a "I need support" function accessible to both panel members and users at all times
- Not design the bilateral thread as a crisis intervention channel in either direction

**Testable prototype:** Implement a persistent "Resources" panel in every thread view, organised by domain, always visible. Measure: rate of user-initiated clicks on this panel. Secondary measure: panel member usage of "share a resource" function. Baseline against platforms with cold-signposting-only approaches where data is available.

### 3.4 Dyadic Privacy Architecture: Specific Crisis Handling Specification

**The structural constraint:** In the eskp.in dyadic model, each panel member operates in a bilateral thread with the user. Neither the platform operator nor other panel members can access thread content. This is a load-bearing design choice. It creates trust. It also means:

1. The platform cannot monitor threads for crisis content
2. A panel member cannot escalate to other panel members without leaving their bilateral context
3. The user's distress in one thread is invisible to panel members in other threads

**Design specifications to address this:**

a. **Persistent in-UI resource access:** Every thread view, for both user and panel member, must display a persistent link/button to the full signposting library. This is always visible, not triggered by content detection. It signals: "we know this can touch hard things; support is always available."

b. **Panel member "flag for support" function:** Allow a panel member to trigger (with user consent) a non-content-sharing notification to the platform: "I've been supporting [user] through something serious and would like to ensure they have additional resources." The platform can then send the user a non-alarming "we want to make sure you have all the support you need" message with the signposting library. No content is shared. This preserves dyadic privacy while enabling an escalation signal.

c. **User "I need more support" trigger:** A persistent user-accessible function that, when activated, sends the user the full signposting library and optionally a message: "Your panel members are here for you. And if you need more specialist support, here's how to access it." Does not notify panel members by default (respects user autonomy).

d. **Panel member onboarding: crisis protocol training:** Before accessing the platform, panel members complete a brief module covering: what to do if a user discloses suicidal ideation or active crisis; the limits of peer support in crisis situations; how to provide warm referral to specialist services from within a bilateral thread; what the "flag for support" function does.

e. **Safe messaging language guidance:** Surfaced to panel members contextually. If a thread contains suicide-adjacent language (detected client-side, not stored server-side), a non-intrusive panel prompt: "Some conversations touch difficult territory. Here's guidance on supportive language: [link to safe messaging resource]."

f. **Emergency override:** The only circumstance in which the platform would consider breaking dyadic privacy is an imminent threat to life. This requires a legal analysis (potentially a Data Protection Act 2018 s.15 basis for processing — vital interests). The platform should not be designed as a crisis intervention service and should not promise emergency response capabilities it cannot deliver. The clearest path is: ensure users understand from onboarding that if they are in immediate danger they should call 999, and that the platform is a peer advisory service, not a crisis service.

## Section 4: Directory Concept Specification

### 4.1 The Vouching Model — Design

A trust-based professional directory built on network vouching differs fundamentally from a review platform in one key property: **a vouch is a private, directed, relationship-mediated endorsement**, not a public rating aggregated from strangers.

The mechanism:
- User A needs a professional in domain X
- The platform asks: "Do any of the people in your trust network have experience with a professional in domain X?"
- If User B (a trusted contact already on the platform) has used Provider P and is willing to vouch for them specifically to User A, a vouch is registered
- User A sees: "[User B], who you trust, has used [Provider P] and vouches for them for [domain X]"
- The vouch is visible only to User A, not publicly
- Provider P is not paying for placement; they are benefiting from a genuine relationship-mediated endorsement
- Provider P may not know a vouch exists unless User B or the platform discloses it

This differs from:
- **Trustpilot / Google Reviews:** Public ratings aggregated from strangers; gameable; providers can pay for enhanced profiles
- **Unbiased:** Adviser-subscription model; advisers pay per enquiry; commercial distortion of the recommendation
- **VouchedFor:** Client reviews are public and aggregated; advisers pay for subscription; reviewer identity is verified but the review is still broadcast, not directed

**The defining property of the vouching model:** The trust signal derives from a pre-existing relationship between the voucher and the recipient of the vouch, not from anonymous crowd aggregation. This is the model that operates naturally when someone asks a trusted friend "do you know a good solicitor?" The platform makes this systematic, searchable, and persistent — but does not alter the fundamental relational basis of the recommendation.

---

### 4.2 Information Architecture

**Data entities:**

1. **Professional Profile** — created by or on behalf of the professional; contains name, credentials, regulated status (FCA/SRA/GMC registration number where applicable, linkable to public register), areas of practice, geographic coverage, contact method. Crucially: no paid placement, no ranking algorithm.

2. **Vouch Record** — contains: voucher user ID; vouchee professional ID; domain of vouch; date; any specific context (e.g., "used for will preparation, thorough and affordable"); visibility flag (visible to specific named recipient only, or available to any platform user who has a trust-graph connection to the voucher). Vouches are not public by default.

3. **Trust Graph** — the platform's representation of who trusts whom, derived from panel membership (your panel members are your trusted network). A vouch from someone in my panel is a first-degree vouch. A vouch from someone in my panel member's network is a second-degree vouch (lower signal strength; should be surfaced as such).

4. **Domain Register** — maps professional profiles to the exclusion and adjacency registers from Sections 1 and 2. Regulated professionals (FCA-authorised, SRA-regulated, GMC-registered) are tagged as such, with linkage to the relevant public register. Non-regulated practitioners in grey-zone domains (coaches, nutritionists, etc.) carry a visible disclosure.

5. **Vouch Request** — a user can ask a specific panel member: "Do you know anyone who could help with X?" The panel member receives this as a task. They can respond with a vouch, with "I don't know anyone for this," or with "I have a suggestion but I'm not comfortable vouching formally."

**Privacy design:**
- Vouches are minimum-data: no financial information, no outcome guarantees
- A professional's listing in the directory does not automatically make them a platform user
- A professional who is listed can request removal at any time (right to erasure applies to their profile data)
- Vouch records between users are personal data of both the voucher and the subject professional; retention policy: retain as long as both the voucher and the user have active accounts; delete on account closure

---

### 4.3 Regulatory Constraints

**Financial advisers:**
- FCA referral fee rules: The FCA does not prohibit referral fees for financial adviser introductions in general, but requires disclosure. COBS 2.3.1 requires a firm to disclose to a client any referral fee or commission received in connection with its service. If the platform receives payment from financial advisers for inclusion in the directory (even a subscription fee), this may constitute a referral arrangement requiring disclosure.
- FCA financial promotions: Any communication that is a financial promotion (inviting someone to engage in investment activity) requires FCA authorisation or approval by an authorised person. Surfacing an FCA-authorised adviser's profile to a user in connection with a financial need may, depending on framing, constitute a financial promotion. **The vouching model partially addresses this:** a vouch from a peer is personal communication, not a mass broadcast. However, the platform's role in facilitating the vouch and making the profile searchable may bring it within scope. This requires specific FCA legal advice. Confidence: low — this is a genuinely uncertain area.
- **Mitigation:** Frame all directory entries as "here is a professional your network knows" rather than "we recommend this professional." Ensure all FCA-authorised advisers' entries link to the FCA register and include the standard "check with FCA register before engaging" prompt.

**Solicitors:**
- SRA referral arrangements: The SRA Standards and Regulations (Code of Conduct for Firms, para 5.1) require solicitor firms to satisfy themselves that any referral arrangements do not compromise their independence or the interests of clients. If the platform refers clients to solicitors and receives payment, this must be disclosed to the client.
- The platform's vouching model (no payment from professionals) sidesteps the referral fee conflict entirely for this regulatory purpose.

**Doctors / GMC-registered professionals:**
- GMC guidance (Good Medical Practice) does not restrict how doctors receive referrals. There are no restrictions equivalent to the SRA or FCA on professional referral arrangements for doctors. The directory is lower-risk in this domain.

**OISC-registered immigration advisers:**
- Immigration advisers listed must be OISC-registered (or solicitors/barristers). The platform must verify this before listing. Display of OISC registration number with link to OISC register is required.

---

### 4.4 Feasibility Assessment

**Trust network density problem:** The directory's value is directly proportional to the density of vouches in the user's trust network. A new user with three panel members who have no vouches on record will see an empty directory. This is the cold-start problem.

**Resolution strategies:**
1. **Bootstrapped vouches:** During early platform growth, allow trusted users (e.g., the founding community) to contribute vouches publicly, acting as a commons of starting vouches visible to all. These are clearly labelled as "community vouches" rather than personal vouches.
2. **Structured vouchable moment:** After any user completes a professional engagement, the platform prompts: "Would you like to vouch for [professional] so others in your network can find them?"
3. **Panel member vouches as first source:** Panel members may be the first source of vouches — they know the user, they may know professionals in their field.

**Confidence:** Medium. The cold-start problem is solvable but requires deliberate design. The directory will be a weak product until the user network reaches a minimum density.

## Section 5: Directory Revenue Models

The constitutional constraint is: any revenue model that creates financial incentives for the platform to prioritise provider interests over user interests is enshittification in waiting.

Doctorow's enshittification framework (2023) describes three stages: (1) platform subsidises users to build critical mass; (2) platform shifts to exploiting business customers; (3) platform harvests value for shareholders. In a professional directory, Stage 2 typically manifests as: paid placement, boosted profiles, sponsored listings, lead generation fees, or subscription fees charged to professionals who want to appear. Each of these creates an immediate distortion of the trust signal: the provider who pays most prominently is not necessarily the provider who performs best.

**Angie's List case study (documented):** Founded 1995 as a subscription-funded, no-advertising model for local service reviews. In 2016, dropped membership fees for users and shifted to an advertiser-funded model. Systematic decline in review quality and provider quality followed, with paid providers receiving preferential placement. Merged with HomeAdvisor in 2017. The trust signal that justified the original product had been monetised away.

**Which? case study:** Remains subscription-funded (consumer-pays model). Substantially maintains editorial independence as a result. Has not undergone the same degradation, though subject to criticism on specific category coverage. The model works because consumers pay for access to the trust signal, not providers.

**Three candidate models:**

---

### Model A: Provider Subscription ("Provider Pays")

**Description:** Providers pay a monthly or annual fee to be listed in the directory. Vouches are still relationship-mediated; listing is not affected by payment level (no paid placement). The fee funds the directory infrastructure.

**Anti-enshittification assessment:** Fails the constitutional test immediately. As soon as providers pay to be listed:
- The platform has a financial incentive to maximise the number of paying providers, which creates pressure to lower verification standards
- The platform has a financial incentive to maintain providers' subscriptions, which creates pressure not to surface negative vouches
- Providers will lobby for premium features (boosted placement, "verified badge" tiers) — these are the first steps of enshittification

**Assessment:** Rejected. This model is enshittification-by-design regardless of good intentions.

**Failure conditions:** Enshittification begins the moment the first provider pays for any feature that affects visibility or reputation presentation. Timeline to enshittification: likely 18-36 months post-launch.

---

### Model B: User Subscription ("Consumer Pays")

**Description:** Platform users pay a subscription fee for access to the platform (including the advisory panel feature and the directory). The directory is a feature of the subscription, not separately monetised. Providers are listed free; inclusion is vouching-dependent only.

**Anti-enshittification assessment:** Passes the constitutional test. Revenue comes from users who benefit from the trust signal. There is no financial incentive to distort the trust signal in favour of providers because providers do not pay.

**Structural risks:**
- User acquisition cost may be high if subscription is required before the directory has sufficient vouch density (chicken-and-egg problem)
- Subscription pricing must be accessible — if only affluent users can afford the platform, the directory reproduces class-based access to professional networks rather than democratising it
- Platform growth pressure creates enshittification pressure indirectly: as the platform grows, investor pressure to monetise providers may emerge even if the founding model is consumer-subscription

**Recommendation:** This is the constitutionally cleanest model. Implement with explicit charter prohibition on provider payment for any directory feature. Include anti-enshittification covenant in founding documents (already in Constitution).

**Failure conditions:**
- Enshittification risk emerges if growth plateaus and the platform faces pressure to add revenue streams. The directory is the most vulnerable feature because providers can plausibly argue "we should pay to be listed because it benefits us."
- Mitigation: constitutional provision that the directory is always provider-payment-free; any amendment requires panel supermajority.

---

### Model C: Separate Free Directory with Platform Cross-Subsidy

**Description:** The directory is entirely free — to users and to providers. It is cross-subsidised by the core advisory platform subscription (if one exists) or by a foundation/grant model in the early phase. The directory is treated as a public good funded by the platform's overall revenue.

**Anti-enshittification assessment:** Strongest constitutional alignment. The directory, as a public good, has no direct revenue attached and therefore no direct enshittification vector. The risk is displaced to the platform's overall revenue model — if the platform enshittifies, the directory loses its funding base.

**Structural risks:**
- Cross-subsidy requires a profitable core platform before the directory can be sustained
- A free directory with no friction creates a different gaming risk: providers may more aggressively seek vouches if there is no cost barrier (though the relational basis of vouching is a natural barrier)
- The directory may become a target for separation from the platform (a well-regarded free directory is an acquisition target that could be spun off and monetised by a third party)

**Assessment:** Theoretically strongest; practically difficult to implement without a profitable core platform first. Recommended as the long-term target model, with Model B as the intermediate model during the growth phase.

**Failure conditions:**
- If the directory is separated from the platform (acquisition or spinout) and subsequently monetised, the trust signal is destroyed
- If the cross-subsidy dries up and the platform is forced to monetise the directory to survive, the constitutional prohibition on provider payment must hold

---

**Recommendation:** Implement Model B (consumer subscription) with the directory as a subscription feature. Commit constitutionally to provider-payment-free directory. Track toward Model C as the platform matures and cross-subsidy becomes viable. Reject Model A categorically.

## Section 7: Academic and Ethical Framework

### 7.1 Theoretical Constructs

**Peer support boundaries — the relational vs clinical axis:**

The foundational academic tension is between peer support as a *relational good* (presence, solidarity, witnessed experience) and peer support as a *clinical intervention* (symptom-targeted, evidence-based, professionally delivered). The risk of conflating these is in both directions:

- **Medicalisation of normal experience:** Treating grief, career uncertainty, relationship difficulty, or financial stress as clinical problems that require professional management, when they are better handled by trusted relationships, is itself harmful. It pathologises ordinary human difficulty and removes it from the domain of community care. (See: Horwitz & Wakefield, *The Loss of Sadness*, 2007; the critique of DSM expansion.)
- **Under-intervention when clinical need is present:** Peer support that substitutes for professional mental health care in acute psychiatric illness, eating disorders at clinical severity, or active suicidal ideation risks harm through omission. The evidence for the boundaries of peer support is clearest at the acute clinical end: peer support alone is insufficient for severe depression, psychosis, or clinical-severity eating disorders (NICE NG222; systematic reviews in *Psychological Medicine*).

The "witnessed reflection vs active advice" framework proposed in Section 2 draws on established coaching theory (Rogers, *Coaching Skills*, 2004; Whitmore, *Coaching for Performance*, 1992) and person-centred therapy (Rogers, *On Becoming a Person*, 1961). The person-centred tradition holds that the helper's role is to create conditions for the person to access their own resourcefulness, not to advise them toward outcomes the helper considers correct.

**Trust, vouching, and reputation in network economies:**

The economics of professional service selection has been extensively studied. Key findings:
- Personal referral is the dominant trust mechanism for selecting lawyers, doctors, accountants, and therapists across cultures (Larsson, *The Rise of Professionalism*, 1977; Nelson & Winter, *An Evolutionary Theory of Economic Change*, 1982 for information asymmetry framing)
- Personal referral reduces information asymmetry more effectively than ratings because the referrer has skin in the game: if the referral goes badly, the relationship between referrer and recipient is damaged (Burt, *Structural Holes*, 1992; social capital theory of referral)
- Digital review platforms have failed to replicate the trust properties of personal referral because they are public, anonymous-aggregated, and gameable (Mayzlin, Dover & Chevalier, *Promotional Reviews*, AER 2014; Luca & Zervas, *Fake It Till You Make It*, 2016, Review of Economics and Statistics)
- The vouching model proposed here aligns more closely with the "strong tie" referral literature than with the "weak tie" discovery literature (Granovetter, *The Strength of Weak Ties*, AJS 1973): strong-tie referrals carry higher trust signal but lower discovery range; the eskp.in directory deliberately prioritises trust signal over discovery range

**Information asymmetry in professional services:**

Arrow (1963, *Uncertainty and the Welfare Economics of Medical Care*, AER) identified the fundamental problem: consumers cannot evaluate professional service quality ex ante or in many cases ex post. This is the classical justification for professional regulation — the licence system is a quality signal when the consumer cannot assess quality directly. The vouching model partially addresses this through vicarious quality assessment: I cannot evaluate the solicitor's competence, but my trusted contact who used them can, and their willingness to vouch is a quality signal I can use.

**Limits of this:** The vouching model reproduces the evaluative competence of the voucher. If the voucher is not competent to assess the professional's quality (they don't know what good financial advice looks like), the vouch may be based on affect (they were nice) rather than quality (they were competent). This is an inherent limitation that should be disclosed in platform documentation.

**Platform ethics and duty of care:**

The academic literature on platform duty of care is actively contested. Key positions:
- Intermediary liability frameworks (derived from the E-Commerce Directive and retained in UK law, now being revised under the DSA-influenced OSA regime) historically provided "safe harbour" for platforms that do not have actual knowledge of illegal content and act expeditiously to remove it when notified
- The OSA 2023 shifts this toward proactive duty of care: platforms are no longer passive conduits; they must actively assess and mitigate risks from illegal and harmful content
- The specific question for eskp.in — whether a platform that facilitates bilateral peer advisory relationships has a duty to intervene when that advice is harmful — is not directly addressed in existing UK caselaw. It is likely to be tested analogically against cases on platform facilitation of harm (for comparison, the 2022 *Royal Borough of Kensington and Chelsea v Meta* and related proceedings on platform liability for algorithmic amplification of harmful content)

**Gatekeeping ethics of signposting:**

There is a meaningful academic critique of professional signposting as a form of gatekeeping that reproduces power asymmetries. When a peer support platform says "this is beyond peer support, see a professional," it may be: (a) genuinely in the person's best interest; or (b) delegitimising the person's difficulty, pathologising normal responses, and deflecting to an inaccessible or unaffordable professional system. (See: Rose, *The Psychological Complex*, 1985; critical psychology literature on medicalisation; Pilgrim & Rogers, *A Sociology of Mental Health and Illness*, 2014.) The platform must navigate this tension by: offering signposting as *addition*, not *substitution*; ensuring the panel member relationship continues even when professional help is also being sought; and never communicating that the user's problem is "too much" for the platform.

### 7.3 UK Policy Literature and Active Research Programmes

**NHS peer support policy:**
- NHS England published *Peer Support and Mental Health: A case for action* (2017) establishing peer support as a recognised NHS intervention
- NHS Long Term Plan (2019) committed to expanding peer support in community mental health settings
- NICE guidance incorporates peer support as a component of stepped care for depression (NG222) and other conditions, explicitly positioning it as complementary to rather than substituting for clinical care

**Online harms and platform duty of care:**
- Ofcom is the primary UK regulator under the OSA 2023. Their codes of practice (published 2024-25) set out specific obligations for user-to-user services, including crisis content management. Ofcom has indicated proportionate enforcement for smaller platforms.
- The Carnegie UK Trust produced extensive research on online harms and platform duty of care (2019-2023) that informed the OSA. Their framing of "systems-based regulation" (regulating platform design rather than content) is directly relevant to the eskp.in architecture.
- The Centre for Mental Health and the Mental Health Foundation have both published on digital peer support and platform design for mental health contexts.

**Active research programmes:**
- Ofcom's Online Safety Research Programme (ongoing, 2024-26) is examining the effects of OSA implementation on user safety outcomes
- The Alan Turing Institute has active research on online trust and platform design (including work on reputation systems relevant to the directory model)
- The University of Edinburgh's Centre for Research on Families and Relationships has published on peer support in digital contexts
- King's College London's Institute of Psychiatry, Psychology and Neuroscience has active work on digital mental health peer support boundaries

**Legal developments to monitor:**
- FCA's Advice Guidance Boundary Review (ongoing 2024-25) may clarify the informal peer guidance boundary for financial matters
- OSA secondary legislation (Ofcom codes) continues to develop — the platform should monitor Ofcom's codes of practice for specific obligations
- Immigration Advice Authority (formerly OISC) regulatory development — the IAA is newly renamed and may update its enforcement approach
