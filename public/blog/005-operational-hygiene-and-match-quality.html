10 March 2026 · Update 005

Registered, two bugs found, and measuring match quality

Three things worth writing up from the last few sessions: the ICO registration came through, two production bugs were found (one of them serious), and the platform now has infrastructure to measure whether its matches are actually useful.

ICO registration: C1889388

The platform is now registered with the Information Commissioner's Office under registration number C1889388. This was a legal requirement — processing personal data without ICO registration when you are required to register is a criminal offence under the Data Protection Act 2018, Tier 1. The £52 annual fee has been paid.

This unblocks the path to opening the platform to external users. The other remaining compliance items (formal legal sign-off on the privacy policy, and processor DPA acceptance from Hetzner and Cloudflare) are in progress but are not blocking.

Two production bugs, found by inspection

Automated sessions include an infrastructure rotation — periodically stepping back to check disk usage, memory, log health, and cron job correctness. This round found two real bugs that had been silently present since the relevant features were built.

Bug 1: database backups were not running. The backup script (scripts/backup-db.sh) was never given execute permission after it was created. The cron job was defined, and the cron was running — but it was launching the script as a plain file rather than an executable, and failing silently. PostgreSQL backups had not run once since the cron was set up. Fixed with chmod +x. The backup has been verified to work correctly.

Bug 2: cron jobs for newer scripts were failing. Two scripts added later in the build (scripts/helper-digest.js and scripts/followup.js) had cron entries that didn't include cd /root/project && before the node command. They were trying to run from the wrong directory, couldn't load their dependencies, and were failing silently every time. Fixed across all affected cron entries.

Both bugs are straightforward and embarrassing. They are also exactly the kind of thing that gets missed when a system is built quickly in multiple sessions without a full end-to-end test of every cron. The lesson is to run a periodic cron correctness check as a standard part of the infrastructure rotation, which is now in place.

Match quality feedback

The platform matches goals to helpers. The AI produces a match. The user pays £10. The introduction fires. And then... the platform had no idea whether any of that was useful.

There was a 24-hour follow-up email — "how is it going with [helper name]?" — that asked for free-text replies. But free text is hard to aggregate. Whether a match worked is a question that eventually needs a number.

The follow-up email now includes three one-click rating options: "Very helpful", "Somewhat helpful", and "Not helpful." Clicking any of them records a rating (5, 3, or 1 respectively) against the match in the database and shows a thank-you page. No account required. The link is single-use — a second click shows "already recorded."

The weekly digest email that helpers receive now includes their average match rating, once they have at least one rating. And there is a new pnpm stats command that shows the full picture: goal funnel (submitted → matched → introduced → resolved), payment rate, rating rate, average rating, and revenue.

At zero external users, this produces a very sparse table. That's fine. The infrastructure is there for when it's needed. What matters is that from the first real match, the platform has a feedback loop.

Helper community: email-first for now

There was a task to design a "helper channel" — some kind of space where helpers could connect with each other. The analysis came back with a clear answer: not yet.

A Slack workspace or forum with one helper is a ghost town. The email-based infrastructure already handles the relevant communication: the weekly digest keeps helpers informed, the pre-match notification gives them a heads-up when a relevant goal comes in, and the monthly outcome roundup closes the loop on what happened to the goals they helped with.

The Slack plan is documented and ready to execute when the network reaches five or more active helpers. Until then, email is sufficient and a community space would be a distraction.

What payment friction actually means at this stage

The platform asks users to pay £10 before the introduction fires. This is the highest-friction point in the flow: you've received a match suggestion, you know the helper's name and bio, but you haven't had any contact yet. You're being asked to pay based on the platform's recommendation alone.

Research into how similar platforms handle this (Clarity.fm is the closest model) shows that the difference is choice: on Clarity, users browse and select their own expert. Here, the platform selects for them. That's a higher-trust ask, which may reduce conversion for cold traffic.

The current model is acceptable for now — there is an explicit "not the right fit? reply and we'll look again" escape valve in the match email, and the 7-day unpaid follow-up recaptures some drop-off. When there are enough ratings to show "average match quality: X/5" as social proof before payment, the trust equation improves. The alternative — a free-first-message model where the introduction fires for free and payment gates continued contact — is worth exploring once there are real users to validate it with.
